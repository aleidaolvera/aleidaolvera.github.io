Chief Keef Hologram Show Shut Down by Police in Chicago

Police Take Matters Into Their Own Hands to Shut Down Fundraiser

A hologram concert was shut down by police July of 2015. The show in Chicago was cancelled after a staff member of Mayor Rahm Emanuel called a performing rapper, Chief Keef, an “unacceptable role model” who “promoted violence. Hammond’s mayor banned Chief Keef from performing in Hammond he told the New York Times “All I’d heard was he has a lot of songs about gangs and shooting people - a history that’s anti-cop, pro-gang and pro-drug use. He’s been basically outlawed in Chicago, and we’re not going to let you circumvent Mayor Emanuel by going next door.”

The concert was actually, a fundraiser for another rapper and his child who had recently been killed in a car crash. The rapper was beamed to the fundraiser in order to perform. Police told members of the audience that they had a right to shut down the concert, because it was on city property.

T.I. and 50 Cent Speak Their Minds About Music Censorship

A group of Urban leaders urged the music industry to censor the words Bitch, Ho, and Nigga from hip-hop albums. The Urban leaders accused hip-hop of influencing violence but didn't accused films showing the same content of influencing violence. Rappers rap about the harsh reality they faced earlier in their life, having to sell dope, not having food to eat, and doing gang related things.

Here is what 50 Cent had to say about this, "Music is a mirror and hip-hop is a reflection of the environment we grew up in, the harsh realities..."

T.I then followed with this, "We look to rappers, athletes and stars to raise our children instead of ourselves. To blame hip-hop when they should be looking in the mirror and blaming themselves ... you know, how much more time could I have been there for my child? I'm on CD. He [the child] listens to me but I can't listen back. I don't know what he's getting from it. You do. You know when you wake up and see him dressed in a shirt you've never seen him in before, and you figure this could be the beginning of something, why don't you stop and talk to him about it. Ask where he got the shirt from. If you don't do that, it will grow into something bigger and bigger."

“And I don't know if you know it or not, people, but there are b------, n-----, and h--- who live in America. And as long as that fact exists, I think rappers deserve the right to talk about it."

Leaders of the Hip-Hop Summit Action Network, want to change hip-hop by asking recording and broadcast industries to voluntarily remove/bleep/delete the misogynistic words “bitch” “Nigga” and “Ho”. Anderson Cooper said that hip-hop popularizes anti-cop feelings and anti-snitching. The Doors & The Ed Sullivan Show

After being invited to play one night on the Ed Sullivan Show, the Doors, famous psych rock band, were asked to change the lyrics of one of their most popular songs to omit possible references to drug abuse. Although the band’s headliner Jim Morrison did verbally acquiesce to this demand, during the live performance the band went ahead and performed the song that night unaltered, earning them a life-time ban from the show, showcasing a classic example of the clash of mainstream and counterculture.

Neil Young and MTV

Neil Young, a noted singer/songwriter, wrote a song called "This Note's for You", which protested the commercialization and product advertisement in songs (written in response to an accident occurring during a Michael Jackson concert advertising Pepsi.) However, MTV refused to play the music video on the channel, citing the references to popular brands, such as "Ain’t singing for Pepsi / Ain’t singing for Coke," as trademark infringement. However, after several argument between the artist and the station, MTV conceded and played the video.

Lady Gaga Forced to Cancel Concert

Lady Gaga was forced to cancel sold-out show in Indonesia after Islamic conservatives protested that her clothes and dance moves would corrupt the youth of the country. The concert was suppose to be held in June 3, 2012 but was opposed by lawmakers and religious leaders. The show was the biggest in her Asian leg of the Born This Way Ball tour with half of the 52,000 tickets sold within the first two hours of sale.

Permits are issued about three weeks before a concert, that is why tickets were sold way in advance. The permit was denied because local police could not guarantee security and public safety. People were outraged claiming that they could separate their own moral values from arts and cultures.
